## 📖 COMPREHENSIVE DOSSIER

### Current roles
Frank E. Buck Professor of Management, Stanford GSB (since 1993); co-directs Exec-Ed programs "Leading Change and Organizational Renewal" (with Harvard's Michael Tushman) and "Culture as a Competitive Advantage"; Professor Emeritus, Berkeley Haas.

### Education & career
- **BS Chemistry, UT El Paso (1965); MBA (1971) and PhD in Organizational Behavior (1975), UC Berkeley Haas.** (Army captain in Vietnam before his MBA.)
- UCLA Anderson (1976–80) → Berkeley Haas (1980–92) → Stanford GSB (1993–). 100+ papers, 6 books. Long-running collaboration with Jennifer Chatman (Berkeley).

### Signature frameworks
**1) Organizational ambidexterity** (with Tushman): simultaneously **explore** (new tech/models/markets; flexibility, tolerance for failure) and **exploit** (refine existing products/processes; efficiency, discipline). Resolve the tension via structural separation + senior-team integration so exploratory units get resources but are shielded from "business as usual." (HBR 2004 "The Ambidextrous Organization," 4,600+ cites; AMP 2013 review.)

**2) The success trap.** Success breeds failure: the systems/metrics/culture that drive current performance become rigid and block discontinuous innovation (Kodak, Boeing). Current customers, metrics, and rewards all favor exploitation.

**3) Culture as competitive advantage** — "a pattern of behavior reinforced by systems and people over time." Five levers: senior-leader modeling; employee involvement; recognition/promotion; stories/symbols; fixing HR systems. Distinguishes normative (strong/consistent) vs. adaptive (flexible/innovative) cultures; strong cultures are most dangerous exactly when they must change.

**4) Narcissistic-CEO research** (with Chatman): narcissistic CEOs with longer tenure extract higher pay and widen pay gaps; reduce collaboration, integrity, and psychological safety — corrosive to the exploration ambidexterity needs.

### Major books & papers
- *Lead and Disrupt: How to Solve the Innovator's Dilemma* (with Tushman, 2016; 2nd ed. 2021) — ideation, incubation, scaling; culture's role.
- *Winning Through Innovation* (1997); *Hidden Value* (with Pfeffer, 2000).
- "The Ambidextrous Organization" (HBR 2004); "Organizational Ambidexterity: Past, Present, and Future" (AMP 2013); narcissistic-CEO compensation work (2014); "Toxic Cultures…" (psychological safety).

### The DaVita case (OB-89) — pre-read
Kent Thiry turned near-bankrupt Total Renal Care (~$200M microcap) into a kidney-care leader (~$7.5B revenue; later ~$16B market cap; 50,000+ "teammates") via a deliberately engineered **"community first, a company second"** culture — "village," "citizens," company song, Three Musketeers ("one for all and all for one"). The **2012 HealthCare Partners merger (~$4.42B)** poses the **culture-extension dilemma**: should DaVita's dialysis culture be exported to a different integrated-care workforce? O'Reilly's framework suggests **shared values, differentiated practices** (export principles like community/service/accountability; let local practices differ).

### Recent focus (2024–2026)
Continued narcissistic-leadership & culture research with Chatman; co-directs LCOR (next Stanford session Sep 13–18, 2026) and "Culture as a Competitive Advantage." *Lead and Disrupt* 2nd ed. (2021) is his most current full statement.

### Distinctive quotes
- Leaders must be "consistently inconsistent."
- Culture change is "unglamorous, daily work."
- "I'd rather have good institutions than good intentions" is Shotts; O'Reilly's analogue: who you **promote** is the strongest signal of what you truly value.

### Likely executive Q&A (grounded)
- **"Do we need an ambidextrous design?"** Only if you face discontinuous change; the failure mode is not noticing the shift from exploitation-dominant to exploration-required.
- **"How do I protect an exploratory unit?"** Structural separation (own P&L/metrics), senior-level sponsor with resource authority, corporate-funded (not core-budget-funded).
- **"We have a strong culture — why change it?"** Strong cultures are hardest to change when the environment shifts; use the five levers.
- **"How do I lead change when my team doesn't see the need?"** Reinforcement beats persuasion — promote people who embody the new behaviors.
- **"Aren't many great CEOs narcissists?"** They attain positions well but erode the collaboration/psychological safety exploration needs.
- **"Should Thiry have exported DaVita's culture to HCP?"** Shared values, differentiated practices.
